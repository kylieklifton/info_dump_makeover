Most of Earth Belongs to No One

By Kylie Clifton

For 170 million years, Earth was just... Earth. No borders, no flags, no one calculating exactly how much of it they could claim. Then we showed up.

We've spent the last few thousand years drawing lines, planting flags, and going to war over who gets to call which patch of dirt "theirs." And after all that? We've claimed less than a third of the planet.

Everything We Fought For

Here's what all that conquest got us.

The top ten countries by landmass control about 28% of Earth's total surface. Russia, the largest country on Earth, owns just 3.34%. The entire landmass of every nation, every empire that rose and fell, every border dispute and treaty negotiation? Thirty percent. That's it.

But Land Wasn't Enough

Starting in the 1970s, countries began claiming the ocean too. Exclusive Economic Zones (EEZs) let nations control waters up to 200 nautical miles from their coastlines. Fishing rights. Drilling rights. Mining rights. Colonialism, extended offshore.

That grabbed another 27% of Earth's surface. Add it to the land, and humanity has drawn lines around 57% of the planet. Which means the other 43%...

Belongs to No One

International waters. The high seas. Bigger than all the land on Earth combined. We call it "lawless" because powerful nations haven't figured out how to divide it yet. No flag. No owner. No rules, except the ones we're only now scrambling to write as corporations eye the seafloor for minerals and fishing fleets scrape it empty.

Forty-three percent of Earth, and it's a free-for-all.

We obsess over territory. We teach children to memorize borders. We fight wars over lines that didn't exist 200 years ago and won't exist 200 years from now. Meanwhile, the majority of the planet, the part that actually regulates our climate, feeds a billion people, and holds ecosystems we've barely studied, belongs to everyone and no one.

And that's probably the problem.
